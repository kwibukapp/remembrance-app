About the Foundation
The Remembrance Foundation is a nonprofit initiative dedicated to strengthening genocide education and digital remembrance in Rwanda. We preserve the history of the 1994 Genocide against the Tutsi through verified testimonies, structured timelines, and ethical AI-assisted learning — built for Rwanda's young generation.

Kwibuka Week — April 7-13
April 7
Official Beginning of Kwibuka
The commemoration week begins with the lighting of the Flame of Hope (Urumuri rw'Ikizere) and laying wreaths at genocide memorials. The Walk to Remember (Urugendo rw'Amaguru) takes place — thousands walk from Parliament to Amahoro Stadium to honor victims and promote unity.
April 8
Role of Media During the Genocide
Reflections on the destructive role of hate media (Uruhare rw'Itangazamakuru). Radio stations like RTLM spread propaganda that fueled violence. This day educates on media responsibility and the power of accurate information.
April 9
Youth and Remembrance
Young Rwandans engage in educational programs, testimonies from survivors, and discussions on preventing future atrocities. Schools hold special assemblies to teach the history of the genocide.
April 10
UN Peacekeepers Withdraw
On this day in 1994, Belgian UN peacekeepers abandoned thousands of Tutsi seeking refuge at Kicukiro Nyanza. Over 2,000 were systematically killed after being left defenseless — a tragic failure of international protection.
April 11
Community Healing
Local communities gather for collective mourning, prayer services, and reconciliation dialogues. Survivors share testimonies while neighbors commit to building a united Rwanda.
April 12
Remembering the Children
Special tributes honor the youngest victims. Educational exhibits show how children suffered during the genocide and emphasize protecting future generations.
April 13
Rebero Memorial — Closing Ceremony
The commemoration week concludes at Rebero Genocide Memorial with a solemn ceremony. Politicians, community leaders, and genocide victims buried at Rebero are honored. Dignitaries lay wreaths as the nation commits to "Never Again."

Genocide Memorial Sites
🏛️
Kigali Genocide Memorial
Kigali · Established 2004
›
The Kigali Genocide Memorial is the final resting place of more than 250,000 victims. Built on the site where thousands were killed, it houses three permanent exhibitions documenting the genocide, colonial history, and the story of children who perished. It is Rwanda's principal national memorial and a UNESCO-recognized site of conscience.
⛪
Nyamata Genocide Memorial
Bugesera · Former Catholic Church
›
A Catholic church where approximately 10,000 Tutsi sought refuge and were massacred. The church has been preserved exactly as it was — clothing of victims remains draped over pews, and bones are carefully laid in the crypt below. A bullet hole in the Virgin Mary statue marks the day the killing began.
🏫
Murambi Genocide Memorial
Gikongoro · Former Technical School
›
Over 45,000 people were killed at the Murambi Technical School in April 1994. Victims had been lured there by local authorities with false promises of safety. Classrooms now contain the preserved remains of thousands of victims — one of the most powerful and sobering memorial sites in Rwanda.
⛰️
Bisesero Genocide Memorial
Kibuye · Resistance Hill
›
Bisesero is unique — it is the site of the longest and most organized armed resistance against the genocide. Thousands of Tutsi fought back for weeks with stones and machetes before being overwhelmed. The memorial honors both the victims and the extraordinary courage of those who resisted. Approximately 50,000 perished here.
🌄
Rebero Genocide Memorial
Kigali · Rebero Hill
›
Located on Rebero Hill in Kigali, this memorial is the burial site of thousands of victims including soldiers and civilians. The site overlooks the capital and serves as a solemn reminder of the genocide's reach into every corner of Rwandan society. It holds mass graves and a remembrance garden.
🏘️
Kicukiro Nyanza Memorial
Kigali · Kicukiro District
›
This memorial marks the site where more than 2,000 Tutsi who had sought refuge at a UN compound were abandoned when Belgian troops withdrew. Left without protection, they were systematically killed. The site is a painful testament to international failure and civilian vulnerability during the genocide.
⛪
Ntarama Genocide Memorial
Bugesera · Former Parish Church
›
At Ntarama church, approximately 5,000 Tutsi were killed in April 1994. The church remains preserved as a memorial — clothes, personal items, and remains of victims are still visible. Ntarama and the nearby Nyamata memorial together document the systematic massacre of almost the entire Tutsi population of the Bugesera region.
🌿
Kiziguro Genocide Memorial
Gatsibo · Eastern Province
›
The Kiziguro memorial stands at a parish site in the Eastern Province where thousands of Tutsi fled seeking sanctuary in the church and were massacred. The site documents the violence that swept through the Eastern Province and honors those whose lives were taken far from the reach of international attention or aid.

What were the main causes of the 1994 genocide?
Colonial-era racial classification by Belgium hardened fluid social identities into fixed categories. Post-independence exclusion, recurring anti-Tutsi violence, and extremist propaganda — especially Radio RTLM — systematically dehumanized the Tutsi population over decades.

About Remembrance App
The Remembrance App is a platform that connects students, researchers, and the global audience to Rwanda's genocide history, ensuring that testimonies and historical narratives are preserved and accessible for education and research.
Despite existing records and testimonies, much of Rwanda's genocide history remains inaccessible to the younger generation. Preserving and centralizing this knowledge is critical for education, remembrance, and reconciliation.
